What this means for India

What is the Stock Market? A Complete Beginner's Guide

Finance Meridian Education·🤖 AI-Assisted·March 20, 2026·8 min read · Beginner
📌 Key takeaway: The stock market is a marketplace where buyers and sellers trade ownership shares in companies. Understanding it is the first step to building long-term wealth.

What exactly is the stock market?

The stock market is a collection of exchanges — like the NYSE, NASDAQ, or India's BSE and NSE — where shares of publicly listed companies are bought and sold every trading day. When you buy a share of a company, you literally own a tiny piece of it.

💡 Simple analogy: Imagine a pizza shop worth ₹10 lakhs split into 10,000 shares at ₹100 each. You buy 100 shares = you own 1% and get 1% of profits.

How do investors make money?

  • Capital appreciation — buying at ₹100, selling at ₹200
  • Dividends — regular cash payments from company profits

How to start investing in India

Open a Demat + trading account with Zerodha, Groww, or Upstox. Takes 30 minutes with your PAN card and Aadhaar. Link your bank account and you're ready to invest.

📊 Historical fact: The Sensex has returned ~12-15% per year over 30 years. ₹1 lakh invested in 1990 would be worth over ₹2 crore today.

How to Buy Bitcoin in India (2026 Guide)

Finance Meridian Crypto·🤖 AI-Assisted·March 20, 2026·7 min read · Beginner
📌 Key takeaway: Buying Bitcoin in India is legal. Gains are taxed at 30%. Use regulated exchanges like CoinDCX, WazirX, or Coinbase. You can start with as little as ₹100.

Is Bitcoin legal in India?

Yes. Crypto trading is legal in India as of 2026. Gains are taxed at 30% with 1% TDS on transactions above ₹10,000. You must report crypto holdings in your income tax return.

How to buy — step by step

  1. Choose an exchange — CoinDCX (India's largest), WazirX (beginner-friendly), or Coinbase (global)
  2. Complete KYC — PAN card, Aadhaar, selfie, bank details. Takes 10-30 minutes
  3. Deposit funds — via UPI, NEFT, or IMPS. Minimum ₹100
  4. Buy BTC/INR — search for Bitcoin, enter your amount, place a market order
💡 Pro tip: Never invest more than you can afford to lose in crypto. Most advisers suggest limiting crypto to 5-10% of your total portfolio.

The 50/30/20 Rule: The Simplest Budgeting Framework That Actually Works

Finance Meridian Personal·🤖 AI-Assisted·March 20, 2026·9 min read
📌 Key takeaway: Split your income into 50% needs, 30% wants, 20% savings. It's the most effective simple budgeting system for most people.

The three buckets

  • 50% — Needs: Rent, groceries, utilities, transport, EMIs, insurance
  • 30% — Wants: Dining out, entertainment, subscriptions, shopping, holidays
  • 20% — Savings: Emergency fund, SIPs, fixed deposits, NPS, investments
💡 Example — ₹60,000 salary: Needs ₹30,000 | Wants ₹18,000 | Savings ₹12,000 → invested in a SIP at 12% annual returns, ₹12,000/month becomes ₹4 crore in 25 years.

How to start today

  1. Calculate your monthly take-home income
  2. Set up an automatic SIP for 20% on salary day — before you spend anything
  3. Pay all fixed bills immediately
  4. Spend the rest guilt-free

How Interest Rates Affect Your Investments (And What to Do About It)

Finance Meridian Analysis·🤖 AI-Assisted·March 20, 2026·6 min read · Intermediate
📌 Key takeaway: When rates rise, bonds fall and growth stocks get hit hardest. When rates fall, the opposite happens. Understanding this helps you position your portfolio intelligently.

Who sets interest rates?

The repo rate in India (set by the RBI) and the federal funds rate in the US (set by the Fed) are the baseline rates for their economies. When central banks raise rates, borrowing becomes more expensive everywhere.

Effect on stocks

Higher rates hurt stocks via higher borrowing costs (reducing profits) and higher discount rates (making future earnings worth less today). Growth stocks (tech, startups) are hit hardest.

Effect on bonds

This is the most direct relationship in finance: when interest rates rise, existing bond prices fall. A bond paying 6% is worth less when new bonds pay 8%.

📊 Practical strategy: Rising rates → shorter-duration bonds, dividend stocks, floating rate funds. Falling rates → long-duration bonds and growth stocks tend to outperform.

What is Dollar Cost Averaging? The Strategy That Removes Emotion from Investing

Finance Meridian Education·🤖 AI-Assisted·March 20, 2026·7 min read · Beginner
📌 Key takeaway: DCA means investing a fixed amount at regular intervals regardless of price. It eliminates the need to time the market and naturally buys more units when prices are low.

How DCA works

You invest a fixed amount — say ₹5,000 — every month, regardless of whether the market is up or down.

📊 Example over 3 months:
Month 1: ₹100/unit → ₹5,000 buys 50 units
Month 2: Market crashes to ₹50/unit → ₹5,000 buys 100 units
Month 3: Recovers to ₹80/unit → ₹5,000 buys 62.5 units

Total invested: ₹15,000 | Total units: 212.5 | Average cost: ₹70.59
Value at ₹80: ₹17,000 — a gain despite the crash!

DCA in India — SIPs

India's Systematic Investment Plans (SIPs) are the world's best DCA implementation. Indians invest over ₹20,000 crore per month via SIPs. Start one with any amount — even ₹500/month — set to auto-debit on salary day and never stop it during market crashes.
